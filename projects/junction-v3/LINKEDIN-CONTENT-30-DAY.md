## POST 8 — The 5-Part Stack
**Type:** Process reveal (long-form teach)
**Best timing:** Tuesday or Wednesday, 7–9am NZ

Every client engagement starts the same way.

Not with ads. Not with content. With a diagnostic.

Then we build in order. Always in this sequence — because the sequence matters.

**Layer 1: Measurement**
You can't improve what you can't see. First 2 weeks are always tracking. Google Ads conversion tracking audited and usually rebuilt. GA4 events verified. Meta pixel validated. UTM structure standardised. Until the measurement is clean, every other decision is a guess.

**Layer 2: Paid acquisition**
Once we can trust the data, we touch paid channels. Google Ads restructured around intent tiers — branded, competitor, category. Meta rebuilt from the audience up: customer list uploads, lookalikes, proper creative testing structure. This is where most of the quick wins sit.

**Layer 3: Email and retention**
Revenue hiding in plain sight. Welcome sequence, abandon cart, post-purchase, win-back. Most businesses have maybe one of these. We build them all — AI-drafted, human-edited, brand-voice checked. Average uplift from email alone: 20–30% of what paid is generating.

**Layer 4: Content and SEO**
Slow burn, but it compounds. AI-assisted content at scale. Article structure, keyword clustering, internal linking. Not spammy — genuinely useful articles that answer the questions your customers are already Googling. Takes 3–4 months to show up. Worth it.

**Layer 5: Automation and AI**
Customer support layer, lead routing, internal reporting. Removes the human from repetitive tasks without removing the human from the decision points that matter.

Most agencies go straight to Layer 2 and stop there.

That's why campaigns work and then plateau.

The most common piece of marketing advice is wrong.

## POST 10 — The Email Number Nobody Talks About
**Type:** Data/insight (short-medium)
**Best timing:** Thursday, 7–9am NZ

Email delivers $36 for every $1 spent.

That's the benchmark figure. Most businesses I work with are getting $4-8.

The gap is almost always the same three things:

List quality. Most email lists have 30–40% cold subscribers who haven't opened anything in 6+ months. Sending to them tanks deliverability and skews every open rate metric you use to make decisions.

Sequence gaps. Welcome email, then monthly newsletters. No abandon cart. No post-purchase. No win-back. Just one touchpoint when there should be seven.

No segmentation. Same email to someone who bought last week and someone who bought 18 months ago. The message that's right for one is wrong for the other.

The return on fixing email isn't complicated math. It's already sitting there in a list you've been ignoring.

## POST 12 — The B2B Leads Problem (Client Situation)
**Type:** Client situation — anonymised diagnosis + fix (long-form)
**Best timing:** Wednesday, 7–9am NZ

A B2B SaaS founder came to me six months ago with a problem I see often.

Leads were coming in. Sales calls were happening. But close rate was sitting at 12%.

They'd already tried: new landing pages, more Google Ads spend, a LinkedIn ads campaign, an SDR.

None of it worked. Close rate didn't move.

Here's what the actual diagnosis looked like:

**Problem 1: Lead quality, not lead volume.**
Their Google Ads was optimised for form fills. Any form fill. Small businesses, students, consultants who wanted a free trial — all counted as "conversions." Sales was spending 60% of their time on leads that were never going to close.

**Problem 2: No qualification layer.**
Between form submit and sales call, there was nothing. No sequence that asked the right questions, pre-sold the value, or filtered out non-fits. Prospects arrived at calls cold.

**Problem 3: Wrong attribution.**
They thought LinkedIn ads weren't working. LinkedIn ads were actually the source of their best leads — but they were converting via Google search after seeing the LinkedIn ad. Last-click attribution was giving Google all the credit.

**The fix:**

Month 1: Rebuilt Google Ads around company size and industry signals. Tightened to job titles that actually buy. Added negative keyword lists that eliminated obvious non-fits.

Month 2: Built a 4-email qualification sequence between form fill and sales call. Emails that asked real questions about use case, team size, and current tools. The replies pre-qualified. The no-replies self-selected out.

Month 3: Implemented proper UTM tracking and viewed-through conversions in Meta. LinkedIn suddenly looked very different.

**Result at 4 months:** Close rate went from 12% to 31%. Same leads. Better quality. Better-prepared conversations.

No new ad spend. No SDR. Just the system working correctly.

## POST 13 — What Breaks When AI Runs Too Much
**Type:** AI reality check (medium)
**Best timing:** Thursday, 12–1pm NZ

I'm going to tell you something that probably sounds like I'm arguing against my own business.

AI breaks when it has no context.

We had a client whose AI customer support layer — which we built and runs brilliantly for 80%+ of queries — started giving wrong answers about a product they'd just reformulated.

The product changed. The AI didn't know. For 11 days, it was confidently telling customers things that were no longer accurate.

Nobody had thought to update the knowledge base.

This is the real challenge with AI systems: they're only as current and accurate as what you feed them. They don't read the memo your ops manager sent on Thursday. They don't know the product changed until someone updates the source.

Human oversight isn't optional with AI. It's structural.

What we do now: every client has a weekly "knowledge sync" — a 20-minute process where we check whether anything in the business changed that the AI needs to know about. Products, policies, pricing, FAQs. Documented, updated, reloaded.

Unglamorous. Non-negotiable.

The businesses that get into trouble with AI aren't the ones that adopt too slowly. They're the ones that adopt and then stop paying attention.

## POST 15 — Google Ads Structure That Actually Compounds
**Type:** Long-form teach (process reveal)
**Best timing:** Tuesday, 7–9am NZ

Google Ads is not complicated. But most accounts are built wrong — and wrong structure compounds the wrong way.

Here's how I think about account architecture:

**The three intent tiers — and why they need separate campaigns:**

**Tier 1: Branded**
People searching your brand name. Highest intent, lowest cost, highest close rate. These should be protected in their own campaign with dedicated budget. Never let branded terms compete with generic terms for budget — they'll almost always lose to higher-volume generic searches and you'll end up paying for brand traffic you'd get anyway.

**Tier 2: Competitor**
People searching your direct competitors. Warm intent — they know what you do, they're comparing. These need different messaging: not "here's what we are" but "here's why we're different." Separate campaign, separate ad copy strategy.

**Tier 3: Category**
People searching what you do, not who you are. "AI marketing NZ," "ecommerce marketing consultant." The hardest and most expensive to convert, but the biggest volume opportunity. These need the most creative testing and the tightest negative keyword lists.

**The mistake most accounts make:**

All three tiers dumped into one campaign, competing for the same budget. Broad match keywords eating the spend. Conversion tracking pointing at anything that looks like a signal.

**What clean structure looks like:**

Separate campaigns per intent tier. Exact and phrase match only (broad match almost always wastes money until you have 12+ months of clean conversion data). Quality Scores targeted above 7 for core terms. Landing pages matched to intent tier — the person who searched "Junction Media" needs a different page than the person who searched "AI marketing consultant NZ."

**Negative keyword lists are underrated:**

Most accounts have 10–20 negative keywords. Good accounts have 200+. Every irrelevant search you block is a click that would have cost you money and told the algorithm you're less relevant.

**The compounding part:**

Clean structure → better Quality Scores → lower CPCs → same budget goes further → more data per dollar → better optimisation decisions → better Quality Scores.

It takes 60–90 days for a restructured account to fully stabilise. Most people don't give it that long. They restructure, see a dip (normal — the algorithm is relearning), and revert. That's where they get stuck.

Run clean structure for 90 days before making any big structural changes.

## POST 16 — The AU Expansion Question
**Type:** NZ market specific (medium)
**Best timing:** Wednesday, 12–1pm NZ

The most common growth conversation I have with NZ founders right now is Australia.

Not if — when and how.

The instinct is usually: "We'll just run the same ads in AU." 

That works for about 3 months. Then it plateaus for the same reasons it plateaued in NZ.

Australia isn't just a bigger New Zealand. The customer acquisition cost is different. The competitive density is different. The search volume is different. Google Ads CPCs in Sydney for competitive categories are 2–3x what they are in Auckland.

What actually works for the AU expansion:

Start in one city. Sydney or Melbourne, not both. You don't have the data or the budget to test multiple markets simultaneously. Pick one, prove the unit economics, then expand.

Localise properly. "NZ business coming to AU" is not a differentiator. Australians don't care where you're from. What's your AU-specific value proposition?

Build your AU email list before you launch. Content strategy, LinkedIn, referrals — 90 days of list-building before you spend a dollar on AU paid media. Cold traffic in a market you don't understand yet is expensive.

The businesses I see get AU right are the ones that treat it like a new country, not a bigger suburb.

## POST 17 — The Agency Model Is Structurally Broken
**Type:** Contrarian take / personal opinion (long-form)
**Best timing:** Monday, 7–9am NZ

This will annoy some people. That's fine.

The traditional marketing agency model is structurally designed to produce mediocre results.

Here's the structure:

A client pays $5k/month. The agency takes on 30 clients to make the economics work. Each client gets 2–3 hours of actual senior attention per month. The rest is junior staff executing templates.

The senior person who sold you the engagement — who impressed you in the pitch — is managing 30 relationships and making strategic decisions on 30 businesses. They're giving each one 2-3 hours of real focus per month.

That's not enough to understand your business. It's barely enough to read the reports.

This isn't a criticism of the people. Most agency people are talented and trying hard. It's a criticism of the model.

The model incentivises:
- Retaining clients, not producing results
- Reporting on metrics that look good rather than ones that matter
- Templates over custom thinking
- Growth in client count rather than depth per client

I'm not saying all agencies are bad. I'm saying the incentive structure produces predictable outcomes — and those outcomes are rarely the compounding growth that founders want.

What's the alternative?

Fewer clients. Deeper integration. Accountability tied to results, not hours.

The math only works if you build an AI execution layer that lets one senior person do the work of a team. That's the model.

Not for everyone. For founders who'd rather have one person who knows their business cold than a team who knows it warm.

## POST 18 — How We Actually Build Email Sequences
**Type:** Process reveal (medium)
**Best timing:** Thursday, 7–9am NZ

Here's the exact process for building an email sequence that converts.

Not theory. What we actually do:

**Step 1: Map the customer journey first**
Before writing a single word, we map every stage — stranger, first touch, lead, first purchase, repeat purchase, lapsed. Each stage gets a different sequence with different objectives. Most businesses only have one sequence. They need five.

**Step 2: Interview the customer**
If we can, we read customer reviews, support tickets, and sales call transcripts. What language do they use about their problem? What objections come up? What made them decide to buy? This shapes copy more than any best-practice guide.

**Step 3: AI drafts, human edits**
We use AI to draft the structure and first pass. This is faster than writing from scratch and surfaces angles we might not have considered. But AI email copy is recognisable — it's too smooth, too generic, too safe. The editing pass puts friction, personality, and specificity back in.

**Step 4: Sequence logic before design**
Who gets this email and when? What happens if they open but don't click? What happens if they buy during the sequence? The trigger logic matters more than the design. A beautifully designed email that sends to the wrong person at the wrong time is worse than a plain-text email that's perfectly timed.

**Step 5: 30 days of live data before optimisation**
Let it run. Don't touch it. Optimise after you have real open and click data — not based on gut feeling.

Most businesses skip steps 1, 2, and 4. That's why their sequences don't convert.

## POST 20 — The Conversion Rate Nobody Wants to Talk About
**Type:** Data/insight (medium)
**Best timing:** Wednesday, 7–9am NZ

Average ecommerce conversion rate: 2–3%.

Average NZ ecommerce conversion rate I see when auditing: 0.8–1.4%.

That gap is not a content problem. It's not an ads problem.

It's a landing page, trust signal, and checkout friction problem.

Think about what that means in practice. If you're spending $10k/month on traffic and converting at 1.2%, you'd generate 50% more revenue — from the same ad spend — if you converted at 2.4%.

That's not a growth strategy. That's a fix.

What causes low conversion rates for NZ businesses specifically:

Speed. NZ is geographically isolated. Image-heavy, unoptimised sites load slowly. Google's Core Web Vitals data suggests the average NZ ecommerce site is slower than AU/US equivalents. Every extra second costs 7–10% of conversions.

Trust signals. NZ customers are appropriately skeptical. Reviews, guarantees, and trust badges matter. Most NZ ecommerce sites treat them as optional.

Checkout friction. Guest checkout, payment options (Afterpay in particular), and address autocomplete — these aren't nice-to-haves. They're the difference between a sale and an abandoned cart.

Before you increase your ad budget — check your conversion rate. The problem might not be at the top of the funnel.

## POST 21 — How I Evaluate a New Client
**Type:** Behind the scenes (medium)
**Best timing:** Tuesday, 12–1pm NZ

Every application I receive goes through the same assessment before I say yes.

It's not about size. It's not about industry. Here's what I'm actually looking for:

**Is the product or service genuinely good?**
I've been offered engagements with businesses that had marketing problems because the product was mediocre. Marketing can't fix a product. I ask for reviews, I look at NPS if they have it, I ask what their refund rate is. If the product isn't solid, I don't take it on.

**Is the founder willing to be wrong?**
Some founders want someone to execute their existing ideas. I need founders who want honest feedback on whether their existing ideas are right. The audits I do often surface things people don't want to hear. If someone needs to be agreed with, I'm not the right partner.

**Is there a real marketing foundation to build on?**
If there's zero data, zero existing customer relationships, zero brand awareness — the engagement takes longer and costs more before it compounds. I prefer clients with 12+ months of operating history. Something to build on.

**Are they operationally stable?**
Marketing amplifies what's already happening. If there are fulfilment problems, customer service disasters, or product supply issues — marketing makes them worse, faster. I need clients who can handle growth.

**Can they move at the right pace?**
Not fast. Steady. Clients who need three approval layers for every email approval slow everything down. The best engagements have one decision-maker who trusts the process.

If all five boxes are ticked, I'll have a deeper conversation. If one is missing, that's the conversation we have upfront.

No cart abandonment sequence.

Average ecommerce cart abandonment rate: 70%. That means 70% of people who added something to their cart left without buying.

## POST 23 — The AI Tools That Disappoint
**Type:** AI reality check (short/punchy)
**Best timing:** Thursday, 7–9am NZ

Honest take on the AI tools that don't deliver what they promise:

**AI SEO content tools** — most produce content that ranks for nothing because it's not substantively better than what already exists. Google rewards expertise and depth, not volume. AI can draft the structure; humans need to add the knowledge.

**AI ad creative generators** — useful for iteration, not for original concepts. The first 10 outputs from any AI image tool are generic. The value is in generation 40 when you've refined the brief enough to get something actually useful.

**AI chatbots from SaaS platforms** — most are rigid decision trees with a chatbot UI on top. Not generative AI. Fine for FAQs. Useless for nuanced questions.

**AI "marketing strategy" tools** — they ask you a few questions and produce a strategy document that reads like a very articulate version of every marketing textbook. Not a strategy. A template.

The tools I'd actually recommend: Claude for drafting and thinking through problems, Perplexity for research, and whatever your ad platform's native AI optimisation is — it's usually underhyped and outperforms manual bidding once you have enough conversion data.

Use the tools. Be honest about what they can't do.

## POST 24 — On Pricing Without Apology
**Type:** Personal/opinion (short/punchy)
**Best timing:** Friday, 7–9am NZ

I don't discount. I don't negotiate monthly fees down. I don't offer short-term trials.

This used to feel uncomfortable. Now it feels necessary.

Here's why:

If I discount to get a client, I've started the relationship with the message that my pricing is negotiable. Every future invoice is a negotiation.

If I offer a trial, I'm signalling I'm not confident the work will pay for itself.

If I reduce scope to fit budget, I'm guaranteeing a worse outcome and setting myself up to be blamed for it.

The clients I've taken at a reduced rate because they "seemed like a great fit" — those are the hardest engagements. The ones where there's the most friction around results, timelines, and expectations.

Price communicates positioning. If your price makes someone pause, good. That pause is part of the filter.

The right client doesn't need you to make it easy. They need to believe it's worth it.

## POST 25 — The Full Customer Journey Audit
**Type:** Long-form teach (process reveal)
**Best timing:** Wednesday, 7–9am NZ

When I audit a business's marketing, I start at the beginning. Not the ads. Not the website. The moment before someone knows you exist.

Here's the full framework I use:

**Stage 1: Discovery**

How does someone find out about you? Search? Social? Word of mouth? Referral? Each channel has a different cost, a different intent signal, and a different conversion rate. Most businesses know which channels they're using. Few know which channels actually produce customers.

Questions I ask: What does your organic search footprint look like? Are you ranking for anything customers are actually searching? What does your social reach look like in terms of real potential customers, not vanity metrics?

**Stage 2: First touch**

Someone hears about you for the first time. What's their experience? If it's a Google search: does your ad show up? Does your organic listing show up? What does the snippet say? If it's social: what does your profile look like to someone who's never seen it?

First touch is where trust starts or fails.

**Stage 3: Consideration**

They're curious. They come to your website. Now what?

What's the page load speed? Is it clear in 5 seconds what you do and who it's for? Do you have social proof above the fold? Is there a clear next step?

Most websites fail at the consideration stage. The product is real, the ads are running, but the website doesn't convert the interest into action.

**Stage 4: Decision**

Something triggers the decision to buy or enquire. For ecommerce: add to cart, checkout flow, payment options. For B2B: contact form, booking page, email reply. 

What friction exists between "I want this" and "I've bought this"? Every step of friction costs you conversions.

**Stage 5: Post-purchase**

This is where most businesses stop paying attention. But the moment after someone buys is when they're most emotionally engaged. The right post-purchase sequence — confirmation, onboarding, check-in, review request, upsell — can increase lifetime value by 40–60%.

Most businesses send one confirmation email and move on.

**Stage 6: Retention and referral**

How do you bring them back? What's your win-back sequence for lapsed customers? What's the referral mechanism?

The cheapest customer acquisition is a happy existing customer who tells someone else.

Running this audit across all six stages tells you exactly where the leaks are. Usually it's not where people think.

## POST 26 — B2B Sales in NZ Is Different
**Type:** NZ market specific (medium)
**Best timing:** Tuesday, 7–9am NZ

NZ is a small market. That changes how B2B works.

In the US or AU, you can run cold outbound at scale because there are enough buyers that a 2% reply rate produces a pipeline.

In NZ, there are maybe 3,000 businesses at the revenue level where your product or service makes sense. Maybe.

Cold outbound at scale burns the list fast. You get one shot at most of the people who matter.

What actually works for B2B in NZ:

Warm network first. New Zealand has a 6-degrees-of-separation problem — it's more like two. The warm introduction from a mutual contact outperforms cold contact by 10x. Before you run outbound, map who your existing clients, friends, and contacts know.

LinkedIn content over cold outreach. If you're showing up consistently with genuinely useful content, the right buyers find you. It takes longer but the lead quality is dramatically higher. When a NZ founder messages you because they've been reading your posts for 3 months — that's a different conversation than a cold DM.

Events and community. NZ tech, startup, and founder communities are small and connected. One good relationship at BNZ Ventures, Creative HQ, or an industry dinner is worth 50 cold emails.

Referrals are the ceiling here. A $3M NZ business growing through referral alone is common. Systems that generate referrals systematically — post-purchase sequences, review requests, referral programs — are underused.

The tactics that work in big markets work differently here. Adjust accordingly.

## POST 27 — What I'd Do Differently Starting a $2M Business Today
**Type:** Personal/opinion (long-form)
**Best timing:** Monday, 7–9am NZ

A founder asked me last week what I'd do differently if I were starting a $2M NZ business today — knowing what I know about marketing.

Honest answer:

**I'd start with email before I started with ads.**

Email is the only marketing channel you actually own. Social platforms change their algorithm. Google changes what it ranks. Ad CPCs go up. Your email list is yours. I'd build it from day one — content, lead magnets, partnerships — before spending a dollar on paid.

**I'd treat content as infrastructure, not output.**

Not "post 3 times a week" — build content that answers the real questions customers have. SEO-structured, genuinely useful, deeply specific. That content compounds for years. The viral post is noise. The article that ranks for the right search term pays you every month.

**I'd hire for operations before I hired for marketing.**

Marketing amplifies what's already happening in a business. If fulfilment, customer service, and product quality aren't solid, marketing makes the problems worse at scale. Get the ops right. Then market.

**I'd obsess over one acquisition channel before adding more.**

Most $2M businesses I work with have spread across 5 channels without being exceptional at any of them. Facebook, Instagram, Google, TikTok, LinkedIn, email, SEO. Pick the one where your customers actually are, and get really good at it before adding channel two.

**I'd set up proper analytics before I set up anything else.**

The number of businesses making decisions based on broken or partial data is staggering. GA4 properly configured. Google Ads conversion tracking verified. Email revenue tracked. Source attribution working. This takes a week and saves thousands.

The businesses I see grow fastest are not the ones doing the most things. They're the ones doing a small number of things correctly.

## POST 28 — Why SEO Takes 4 Months (and Why It's Worth It)
**Type:** Process reveal / data-insight (medium)
**Best timing:** Thursday, 12–1pm NZ

SEO is the only marketing channel where the results outlast the investment.

Paid ads: stop paying, stop getting traffic. Email: stop sending, the list goes cold. SEO: build it properly and the traffic compounds for years.

Here's why it takes 4 months and why that's actually the point:

**Month 1:** Keyword research. Understanding what customers are actually searching, not what we think they're searching. Site audit — fixing technical issues that block indexing. Optimising existing pages. No new content yet.

**Month 2:** First articles go live. Long-form, properly structured, genuinely useful. Not written for the algorithm — written for the human who has the problem. AI drafts the structure and first pass; we add expertise and specificity.

**Month 3:** Internal linking structure built. Existing pages start picking up from the technical fixes. First articles begin showing up for lower-competition terms.

**Month 4+:** Compounding starts. Articles that ranked 30th move to page 2. Articles on page 2 start hitting page 1. Traffic from organic search grows without any additional spend.

**Month 6:** Organic traffic is producing leads or sales from content that existed 4 months ago. That content continues to work with no ongoing spend.

The reason most businesses don't get SEO results: they stop at month 2 when they don't see month-6 results.

The businesses with the best SEO footprints started 2 years ago. The second best time to start is now.

## POST 29 — Brand Is Not What You Think It Is
**Type:** Contrarian take (short/punchy)
**Best timing:** Friday, 12–1pm NZ

Founders think brand is their logo, their colours, their tone of voice guide.

That's not brand. That's a style guide.

Brand is what someone thinks and feels when they encounter your business.

Which means brand is shaped by:
- Whether your product works as promised
- How your customer service handles the problem
- What your customers say to each other when you're not in the room
- Whether you show up consistently with something worth reading
- How your invoice emails are worded

Design is one input into brand. Experience is most of it.

I've seen businesses with beautiful visual identities and terrible brand. The design is there. The experience is broken.

I've seen businesses with functional, unpolished design and incredibly strong brand. Because their product is exceptional and their customers are evangelists.

Spend on experience before you spend on brand identity. Every time.

## POST 30 — The Businesses That Will Win the Next 5 Years
**Type:** Personal/opinion (long-form)
**Best timing:** Tuesday, 7–9am NZ

I think about this more than anything else right now.

Not which industries will grow. Which kinds of businesses will pull ahead — in any industry.

Here's what I'm observing:

**The winners will have better data infrastructure.**

Not bigger data. Better data. Clean tracking, proper attribution, email lists they own, customer behaviour they understand. The businesses making decisions on clean data will consistently outperform the ones making decisions on gut feeling or vanity metrics.

**The winners will be smaller and more capable than expected.**

AI is lowering the staffing requirements for marketing output. A 3-person team with the right systems can produce what used to need 15 people. The lean, AI-native operator will outmanoeuvre the bloated team on speed and cost.

**The winners will have compounding assets.**

Not paid traffic — owned assets. Email lists. SEO rankings. Genuine brand reputation. Community. These assets take years to build and are nearly impossible to replicate quickly. The businesses investing in them now are building a moat.

**The winners will know their customer better than their competitors.**

Not because they have more data — because they're paying closer attention. Customer interviews. Support ticket analysis. Review mining. The business that understands its customer most deeply will make better product, marketing, and messaging decisions than any AI tool can optimise into.

**The winners will move at a different pace.**

Not recklessly fast. Deliberately fast. They'll test quickly, learn quickly, and cut what isn't working before it becomes a sunk cost. The businesses that take 6 months to make a decision on a new channel are already behind.

None of this requires a big budget. It requires building habits and systems.

The NZ businesses I see pulling ahead aren't the best-resourced. They're the ones treating marketing like an operational discipline rather than an occasional spend.
