Most NZ trades businesses rely on two things: word-of-mouth and hoping the phone rings. That worked fine for a decade. It's not enough anymore.

Here's the reality: when a homeowner in Remuera needs a plumber at 7pm, they open Google, type "plumber near me," and call the first business with solid reviews and a working website. They don't ask their neighbours. They don't check their phonebook. They search.

If you're not showing up at that moment — with the right positioning, the right reviews, and an easy way to contact you — you're handing the job to whoever is.

This is the playbook for NZ trades and construction marketing in 2026. No fluff, no generic advice — just what actually works in this market.

## Start With Local Search, Not Ads

The single most valuable thing a NZ trades business can do is dominate local search results. Not paid ads — organic local search, specifically Google Maps.

When someone searches "builder Tauranga" or "emergency electrician Hamilton," Google shows a map pack of three local businesses. These three get the majority of clicks. Everything below them is fighting for scraps.

Getting into that map pack is not complicated, but most trades businesses ignore it:

**1. Claim and fully optimise your Google Business Profile**

This is free and takes an afternoon. Fill out every field: services, service areas, business hours, photos (real photos of your work, your team, your vehicles), your description, your categories. A half-complete profile is invisible. A fully optimised profile with regular posts and fresh photos outranks lazy competitors.

**2. Build local citations**

Citations are mentions of your business name, address, and phone number across directories — Neighbourly, Yellow Pages NZ, Finda.co.nz, Builderscrack, local council directories. Consistency matters. The same NAP (Name, Address, Phone) across all listings signals legitimacy to Google.

**3. Get reviews consistently**

I'll cover this properly in a separate section. Reviews are the biggest ranking factor in Google Maps after proximity. Ten reviews beats zero reviews every time, regardless of how beautiful your website is.

## Google Ads: High-Intent Capture

For trades, Google Search Ads are the right paid channel — not Facebook, not Instagram, not display. Why? Because trades are intent-driven. People search Google when they have a problem right now. They browse Instagram when they're relaxed and not in buying mode.

**Emergency and urgent queries are the gold:**

- "Emergency plumber Auckland"
- "Hot water cylinder replacement Christchurch"
- "24 hour electrician Wellington"
- "Gas fitter urgent Auckland"

These searches come from people who need a tradesperson today, often within hours. The conversion rate on these queries is high and the lifetime value of a new client relationship from a well-handled emergency job is significant.

**Project queries are the pipeline builders:**

- "New build builder Tauranga"
- "Kitchen renovation Auckland cost"
- "Deck builder Hamilton quote"
- "Bathroom renovation contractor Dunedin"

These are people in planning mode. They're collecting quotes. If you show up here with a clear, professional presence and a compelling offer (free site visit, fixed-price quote), you're in the running.

**Campaign structure matters**

Don't run one campaign targeting everything. Structure by service type and intent level. Emergency queries should go to a page designed for urgency — fast contact options, phone number prominent, trust signals front and centre. Project queries should go to a page that builds confidence and makes it easy to request a quote.

Track every call and form submission back to the specific keyword and ad that generated it. After 90 days, you'll know exactly which keywords drive profitable jobs and which are wasting budget.

## Review Generation: The System You're Missing

Online reviews are word-of-mouth at scale. A trades business with 47 five-star reviews wins the enquiry before price is even discussed. Most NZ trades businesses have fewer than 10 reviews — not because they do bad work, but because they never ask.

**The ask is the entire strategy.**

After every completed job, send a follow-up message:

> "Hey [Name], great to work with you on the project. If you have two minutes, a Google review would mean a lot — here's the direct link: [link]. Takes about 2 minutes and helps other homeowners find us. No pressure either way."

That's it. Done via text, WhatsApp, or email. Personalised, not automated-feeling.

Over 12 months, a trade with 3-5 jobs per week that asks every client for a review will accumulate 50-100 reviews. That profile dominates local search, dominates the consideration phase, and dramatically reduces the friction between "found you online" and "booked."

## Seasonal Campaigns: Stop Winging It

NZ trades have predictable seasonal patterns. Most businesses know this intuitively but don't plan marketing around it. That's a missed opportunity.

**Renovation and outdoor projects (September–February):**

New Zealand's spring-summer period is renovation season. Deck builders, landscapers, painters, and pool installers should be scaling ad spend and content from August onwards — capturing homeowners who are planning their summer project. The best jobs go to the businesses already in their consideration set before they start seriously collecting quotes.

**Heating and winter comfort (April–July):**

Heat pumps, insulation, fireplaces, and hot water systems sell in the cold. Winter urgency for heating repairs is immediate. Run awareness campaigns in autumn before the first cold snap so you're the first call when people realise their heating isn't working.

**Pre-Christmas push (October–November):**

Homeowners who want renovations completed before Christmas start planning in October. This is high-value, time-sensitive territory. If you're a builder or interior trades business, October-November paid campaigns targeting "kitchen renovation before Christmas" or "bathroom renovation Auckland" can fill your Q1 pipeline.

Build a 12-month campaign calendar. Know when to scale, when to hold, and what message fits each season.

## Commercial Construction: A Completely Different Game

If you're a commercial builder, structural engineer, or specialist subcontractor, everything above still applies — but the B2B layer is what actually builds your pipeline.

Commercial construction buyers — developers, project managers, architects, property investors — don't find you on Google Maps. They find you through:

- **LinkedIn** — where procurement decisions happen in the professional development world
- **Industry associations** — NZIOB, NZIA, Registered Master Builders
- **Referral from other professionals** — architects recommending builders, builders recommending subbies
- **Content credibility** — case studies, project showcases, thought leadership that demonstrates expertise

**LinkedIn for commercial construction:**

A consistent LinkedIn presence — company updates, project highlights, team content, and commentary on NZ construction industry issues — builds the credibility that wins commercial conversations. The developer who's seen your LinkedIn posts for six months is far more likely to reach out than the company they've never heard of.

Targeted LinkedIn outreach to developers and project managers in your region is legitimate business development. Done correctly — not spam, but value-first connection building — it opens doors that cold calls don't.

**Case studies and project showcases:**

Commercial clients want to see what you've built. Not a gallery of photos, but a story: the challenge, the approach, the outcome. Written case studies on your website, combined with LinkedIn posts referencing them, build a body of evidence that your competitors aren't creating.

## The One Thing Most NZ Trades Businesses Miss

Every NZ trades business I talk to underestimates how much the digital impression of their business matters before the first phone call is ever made.

By the time a prospective client calls you, they've already looked you up. They've checked your Google reviews. They've glanced at your website. They've maybe looked at your Facebook or Instagram. In 30 seconds, they've formed a strong opinion about whether you're the kind of company they want working in their home or on their project.

Most trades websites are outdated, hard to use on mobile, and don't answer the basic questions a prospective client has: What do you do? Where do you work? Are you licensed? What does your work look like? How do I contact you?

Fix the basics. A fast, mobile-friendly website with clear service pages, real photos of your work, your credentials prominently displayed, and a single obvious contact CTA does more for your conversion rate than any ad campaign.
