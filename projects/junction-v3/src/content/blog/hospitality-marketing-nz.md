New Zealand's hospitality industry is one of the country's most competitive — and most under-marketed. The best restaurants in Auckland, the best hotels in Queenstown, the best venues in Wellington: many of them are invisible online.

Meanwhile, mediocre competitors who've figured out Google Business Profile and Meta Ads are taking bookings they don't deserve.

## The Foundation: Google Business Profile

If you do nothing else from this article, do this: audit your Google Business Profile and make it excellent.

Google Business Profile (formerly Google My Business) is the single most important marketing asset for most NZ hospitality businesses. When someone searches "restaurants near me" or "best café in Wellington" or "hotels in Queenstown," Google shows a map pack with 3 local businesses. Being in that map pack — or not — can be the difference between full bookings and empty tables.

### What an excellent GBP looks like:

**Complete information:** Every field filled in. Hours (including holiday hours), menu or services, website, phone number, address, categories. Incomplete profiles rank lower and lose trust with potential customers.

**Photos that sell:** Not stock photos. Real photos of your space, your food, your rooms, your team. Google weights profiles with more photos higher. Professional photos are worth the investment — a poorly lit photo of your best dish is costing you bookings.

**Regular posts:** Google Business Profile has a posting feature that most hospitality businesses ignore. Posting 1–2 times per week — specials, events, seasonal updates — signals activity to Google and gives potential customers a reason to choose you.

**Q&A management:** People ask questions on your GBP. If you don't answer them, your competitors' customers answer them (or no one does, and the customer goes elsewhere). Check and answer your Q&A monthly.

**Booking integration:** If you use a booking system (Sevenrooms, ResDiary, Bookeo, etc.), integrate it with your GBP so customers can book directly from Google. Reducing friction at the point of discovery directly increases conversion.

## Reviews: The Social Proof Engine

Reviews are the second most important ranking factor for local hospitality businesses on Google. And they're the single most influential factor in consumer decision-making for restaurants, hotels, and venues.

A hospitality business with 200 Google reviews and a 4.4 average will almost always beat a competitor with 40 reviews and a 4.8 average for new customer acquisition — even though the lower-reviewed business has a "better" rating. Volume matters. Recency matters. Responding matters.

### Building a review system

The best hospitality operators have a system for generating reviews, not a hope.

**The simplest system:** At the end of a positive interaction — the end of a meal, check-out, after an event — a staff member asks for a review. Not "could you leave us a review?" but "If you enjoyed your experience, we'd really appreciate it if you could leave us a Google review. It takes about 30 seconds." Combined with a QR code or short URL, this converts.

**SMS/email follow-up:** For businesses with booking systems that capture contact details, an automated SMS or email 2–4 hours after a visit asking for feedback (with a direct Google review link) is highly effective. A 10–15% conversion rate from these automations is realistic.

**Responding to every review:** Responding to reviews — positive and negative — signals care to prospective customers and tells Google your business is active. Your response to a negative review is often more persuasive than the review itself. Be genuine, be specific, don't be defensive.

## Local SEO for Hospitality

Google Business Profile is local SEO — but it's not all of it. Your website's local SEO matters too.

### Key areas:

**On-site location signals:** Your website should clearly mention your suburb, city, and region. Not just in the contact page — in the copy throughout the site. "Wellington waterfront restaurant" not just "restaurant." Search engines look for these signals to understand where and what you are.

**Location-specific landing pages:** If you operate multiple locations, each location needs its own dedicated page. A single "Locations" page with all your Auckland outlets listed doesn't give Google enough signal for each individual location.

**Local content:** Writing about local events, nearby attractions, or your neighbourhood builds local relevance. A Queenstown hotel writing about local hiking trails or seasonal lake activities will rank for those search terms and attract guests who are researching the destination.

**Schema markup:** Adding LocalBusiness schema markup to your website helps search engines understand your business details precisely. It's a small technical change that delivers clear SEO benefit.

### The "best [category] in [city]" opportunity

These searches — "best sushi Auckland," "best boutique hotel Wellington," "best wedding venue Hawke's Bay" — have high commercial intent and are winnable for well-optimised hospitality businesses. A combination of strong GBP, quality reviews, website authority, and on-site content targeting these terms can get a hospitality business ranking well for its category in 6–12 months.

## Social Media for NZ Hospitality

Instagram and Facebook remain the dominant social platforms for hospitality marketing in NZ. TikTok is growing fast — particularly for younger demographics and for restaurants, which get disproportionate organic reach on the platform.

### What works on social:

**Consistency beats virality:** A hospitality business posting 3–4 times per week every week will outperform one that posts 20 times in a burst and then goes quiet for two months. Algorithms and audiences both reward consistency.

**Show the reality, not the brochure:** The highest-performing hospitality content on Instagram and TikTok in 2026 is authentic and specific — behind-the-scenes kitchen content, staff faces, the story of a dish, the view from room 14 on a clear morning. Generic glossy content performs worse than it did three years ago. Real content wins.

**Video first:** Reels (Instagram) and TikTok videos consistently get more reach than static images. A 20-second video of a dish being plated will outperform a professional photo of the same dish. You don't need a production crew — a good phone and good natural light is enough.

**User-generated content:** Customers posting about their experience at your venue is the most trusted form of content there is. Encouraging it (a good hashtag, reposting stories, asking permission to share) builds a library of authentic content at zero cost.

## Meta Ads for Hospitality

Facebook and Instagram ads work well for hospitality businesses — particularly for specific, time-bound campaigns: fill Monday–Wednesday covers that are chronically empty, promote a new menu launch, drive awareness of your function and events business.

### What converts in hospitality Meta Ads:

**Highly visual creative:** Hospitality ads live or die on the visual. A stunning food photo or a 15-second video of your space will outperform text-heavy creative every time. Invest in creative quality.

**Tight geographic targeting:** Most hospitality businesses serve a local audience. Target 5–15km radius of your location for restaurant and café campaigns. For hotels and venues, you can go broader — target domestic travellers by targeting major NZ cities other than your own, plus relevant interests.

**Clear, frictionless CTAs:** "Book Now" linked directly to your booking page. "Get Directions" for foot-traffic campaigns. Reduce friction between the ad and the action you want.

**Retargeting your warm audience:** People who've visited your website, engaged with your Instagram, or watched your video content are your warmest prospects. A retargeting campaign targeting these audiences will always outperform cold audiences at lower CPCs.

**Lookalike audiences:** If you have a customer email list of 500+ people, Meta can find people who look like your existing customers. This is often the best-performing cold audience for hospitality businesses.

### What to avoid:

- Boosting posts instead of running proper campaigns (boosted posts have poor targeting and limited optimisation)
- Generic creative ("Come try our new menu!")
- Not tracking conversions properly — if you can't measure bookings or foot traffic from Meta Ads, you can't optimise

## AI Tools for Hospitality Marketing

The hospitality businesses building the strongest online presence in 2026 are the ones systematising their marketing with AI. Practically, this means:

**Review response automation:** AI can draft review responses at scale — so you're responding to every review within 24 hours rather than once a month when someone finds time. The drafts still get human review before posting, but the lift is significant.

**Social caption generation:** Consistent posting is the hardest part of social media for time-poor hospitality operators. An AI-assisted workflow — where you supply the photos and a few words about what's shown, and the AI generates caption options — makes weekly content creation 30 minutes instead of 3 hours.

**Ad creative testing:** Generating 8–10 ad variants for testing rather than 2 is now achievable for a small marketing team. More variants means faster learning, better performance, lower CPCs over time.

**Reservation and inquiry handling:** AI chatbots on your website that can answer common questions, check availability, and capture inquiry details are increasingly affordable and easy to set up. A potential customer asking about private dining at 11pm on a Saturday shouldn't have to wait until Monday morning for a response.

## Bringing It Together: The Hospitality Marketing Stack

A well-functioning hospitality marketing operation in NZ in 2026 looks like this:

1. **Foundation:** Excellent Google Business Profile, 50+ recent reviews, consistent responding
2. **Website:** Clear local SEO signals, location-specific content, fast load time, booking integration
3. **Social:** 3–4 posts/week on Instagram (plus TikTok if you have the capacity), consistent brand voice, UGC programme
4. **Paid:** Ongoing Meta retargeting campaign to your warm audience + occasional campaign bursts for specific objectives
5. **Email:** Post-visit automation for review generation + periodic newsletter to your subscriber base
6. **Analytics:** Google Analytics 4 and GBP Insights reviewed monthly — know what's driving bookings

None of this is rocket science. But most NZ hospitality businesses have maybe two or three of these running at any quality level. The ones running all of it properly are consistently booked out when their competitors are not.
