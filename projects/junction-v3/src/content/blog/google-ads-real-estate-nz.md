# Google Ads for Real Estate NZ: How to Generate Quality Leads in 2026

In New Zealand's property market, timing is everything. Buyers and vendors don't leisurely browse — they search with urgency. "Houses for sale Havelock North", "sell my house Auckland fast", "best real estate agent Wellington" — these are high-intent searches from people ready to make decisions worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Google Ads puts your agency or personal brand in front of those people at exactly the right moment. Done well, it's one of the most cost-effective lead generation tools available to NZ property professionals. Done poorly, it burns budget on clicks that never convert.

This guide covers how to run Google Ads for real estate in New Zealand in 2026 — from keyword strategy to landing page design to budget allocation.

---

## Why Google Ads Works for NZ Real Estate

The NZ property market has some unique characteristics that make paid search particularly effective:

**Search intent is transactional.** Someone searching "3 bedroom house for sale Tauranga" isn't doing research — they're looking to buy. That intent translates directly into leads when you have the right campaign structure.

**The geographic targeting is precise.** You can target by suburb, city, or region. If you operate in East Auckland, you can show ads exclusively to people searching within those suburbs — not wasting budget on Christchurch buyers you can't serve.

**Competition varies by region.** Auckland and Wellington are competitive. Provincial cities like Whanganui or Gisborne have lower CPCs, meaning leads cost significantly less. If you're a regional agency, Google Ads can be exceptionally cost-effective.

**The lifetime value of a deal is enormous.** A $1.2M home sale at a 2.5% commission rate is $30,000. If your Google Ads campaign generates 20 qualified leads per month at $50 each, that's $1,000 in ad spend against a potential $30,000+ commission. The maths works.

## Keyword Strategy for Real Estate Google Ads NZ

Real estate Google Ads in NZ typically uses three keyword categories:

### 1. Buyer Keywords
These target people looking to purchase property:
- "[suburb] houses for sale"
- "homes for sale [city]"
- "property for sale [region]"
- "first home buyer [city]"
- "investment property [city]"
- "lifestyle blocks [region]"

### 2. Vendor Keywords
These target people considering selling:
- "sell my house [city]"
- "real estate appraisal [suburb]"
- "how much is my house worth [city]"
- "best real estate agent [city]"
- "real estate agent fees nz"
- "property valuation [city]"

### 3. Brand + Competitor Keywords
- Your agency name (brand protection)
- Competitor agency names (conquest)
- "Ray White vs Bayleys" type comparison queries

**Pro tip:** Vendor keywords typically generate higher-value leads than buyer keywords. A single vendor listing can generate commission and referrals for years. Prioritise vendor intent in your campaigns.

## Match Types and Negative Keywords

For real estate NZ campaigns, we recommend:

**Phrase match** for most keywords. This gives you control while still capturing relevant variations. "Houses for sale Auckland" on phrase match will trigger for "affordable houses for sale Auckland" but not "houses for sale Sydney."

**Exact match** for your highest-converting keywords. Once you've identified which specific searches drive leads, lock them in with exact match to maintain control.

**Negative keywords are critical.** The real estate category has significant irrelevant traffic you need to filter out:
- "rent" / "rental" / "to rent" (unless you also do property management)
- "commercial" (unless you offer commercial property services)
- "overseas" / "foreign" / "international"
- "DIY" / "FSBO" / "sell privately"
- Competitor suburb names you don't cover

Build your negative keyword list before launch and review search term reports weekly in the first month.

## Ad Formats That Work for Real Estate

### Responsive Search Ads (RSAs)
Your primary format. Provide 15 headlines and 4 descriptions — Google will automatically test combinations to find what performs best. For real estate:

**Headlines to include:**
- "[City] Real Estate Experts"
- "Free Property Appraisal"
- "Sell Your Home for More"
- "[X] Homes Sold in [City]"
- "Local Agents Who Know [Suburb]"
- "No Sale, No Commission"

**Descriptions to include:**
- "Our local agents know [city] property inside out. Free appraisal, no obligation."
- "X homes sold in [city] last year. See what your property could fetch today."
- "Thinking of selling? Get a free appraisal from [city]'s top-rated real estate agency."

### Performance Max Campaigns
Google's Performance Max campaigns now appear across Search, Display, YouTube, Gmail, and Maps. For real estate, PMax can be particularly effective for remarketing to website visitors and reaching buyers earlier in their research phase.

Use PMax alongside your core Search campaigns, not as a replacement.

## Landing Pages: Where Real Estate Campaigns Win or Lose

Your ad copy gets the click. Your landing page gets the lead. Most real estate Google Ads campaigns underperform because of poor landing pages, not poor ads.

**What a high-converting real estate landing page needs:**

**A clear, singular offer.** "Get a free property appraisal" or "View homes for sale in [suburb]" — not a generic homepage with six different calls to action.

**Local proof.** Number of homes sold in the area, testimonials from local clients, photos of sold properties in recognisable suburbs. NZ buyers and vendors respond to local credibility far more than national brand names.

**Fast load speed.** Google's Core Web Vitals affect your Quality Score, which affects your CPC. A slow landing page costs you money twice: lower conversions and higher ad costs.

**A simple lead form.** Name, email, phone, and one qualifying question (e.g., "Are you looking to buy, sell, or both?"). Every additional field drops conversions.

**Click-to-call on mobile.** Real estate is a high-consideration purchase. Many motivated prospects would rather call than fill a form. Make your phone number tappable.

## Budget Allocation and Bidding Strategy

**Starting budget:** For most NZ real estate agencies, we recommend starting with $2,000–$4,000 per month. This gives Google's algorithm enough data to optimise while limiting risk.

**Bidding strategy:** Start with Maximise Clicks to gather data, then switch to Target CPA (cost per acquisition) once you have 30+ conversions recorded. For real estate, a reasonable target CPA is $80–$150 per qualified lead.

**Dayparting:** Real estate searches spike in evenings and weekends when buyers have time to research. Consider increasing bid adjustments (+20–30%) during these hours.

**Device bidding:** Mobile devices generate the majority of searches, but desktop users often convert better (longer sessions, more detailed browsing). Watch your device-level conversion data and adjust bids accordingly.

## Tracking and Measurement

You can't optimise what you don't measure. Set up:

**Google Ads conversion tracking** for form submissions, phone calls (using call tracking numbers), and appointment bookings.

**Google Analytics 4** linked to your Google Ads account to track on-site behaviour after the click.

**CRM integration** to track which leads actually become clients and what revenue they generate. This is the data that tells you your true ROI — and which campaigns to scale.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Sending traffic to your homepage.** Your homepage is for everyone. Your ad is for a specific person with a specific intent. Build dedicated landing pages.

**Ignoring Quality Score.** Ads with poor relevance cost more per click. Align your keywords, ad copy, and landing page content for maximum Quality Score.

**Not excluding irrelevant searches.** Check your Search Terms report weekly. You'll find searches that have nothing to do with your business — add them as negatives immediately.

**Setting and forgetting.** Google Ads requires active management. Weekly optimisation is the minimum. If you're not reviewing data and making adjustments, you're leaving money on the table.
