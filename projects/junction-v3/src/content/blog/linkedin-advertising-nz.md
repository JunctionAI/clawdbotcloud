LinkedIn advertising in New Zealand is one of the most underused B2B acquisition channels in the market. Most NZ businesses either avoid it entirely ("too expensive") or run campaigns that generate impressions and no pipeline. Neither approach is right.

When LinkedIn Ads are run correctly — with the right targeting, the right creative format, the right offer, and proper attribution — they are one of the most efficient ways to reach decision-makers at target companies in New Zealand. No other platform lets you target by job title, company size, industry, seniority, and skills simultaneously.

This guide is for NZ B2B businesses that want to understand whether LinkedIn advertising is right for them, and if so, how to run it properly.

## Why LinkedIn Advertising Is Different From Other Paid Channels

Before discussing tactics, it is worth being clear on what makes LinkedIn structurally different from Google or Meta — and why that matters for how you run campaigns.

**Professional intent context.** When someone is on LinkedIn, they are in a professional mindset. They are thinking about work, their career, their business. This means B2B messages land with significantly less friction than on Facebook or Instagram, where the same person is in leisure mode.

**Precision B2B targeting.** LinkedIn's targeting data — job titles, seniority levels, company sizes, industries, skills — is self-reported and updated by users themselves. It is significantly more accurate than third-party demographic data. For NZ B2B businesses targeting specific job roles or company profiles, this precision is unmatched.

**Higher cost, higher quality.** LinkedIn CPCs in New Zealand typically run $8-20 NZD, compared to $1-5 on Meta. This makes LinkedIn look expensive on a per-click basis. The comparison that matters is cost per qualified lead or cost per pipeline opportunity — and on that metric, LinkedIn often outperforms cheaper channels because the targeting eliminates much of the waste spend on irrelevant audiences.

**Longer consideration cycles.** LinkedIn works best for B2B products and services with 1-6 month sales cycles and average deal values of $10,000+. If your average customer is worth $500 and closes in a week, the economics of LinkedIn advertising rarely work. If your average customer is worth $50,000 and the sales process takes 3 months, LinkedIn is worth serious investment.

## Is LinkedIn Advertising Right for Your NZ Business?

LinkedIn Ads make sense when you can answer yes to most of these:

- Your ideal customer is a professional or a business owner
- You know the specific job titles or seniority levels you are targeting
- Your average customer value is $5,000+ (ideally $20,000+)
- You have a compelling offer for cold audiences (not just "buy our product")
- You have a follow-up system (email nurture, sales outreach) to work the leads generated

If you sell direct to consumers, have low average transaction values, or lack a B2B sales infrastructure to work warm leads, LinkedIn advertising is probably not your best investment. The [social media NZ](/services/social-media-nz) channel landscape is worth evaluating more broadly first.

## Campaign Structure: How to Set Up LinkedIn Ads in NZ

### Objective First

LinkedIn campaigns are structured around objectives. For most NZ B2B businesses, the useful objectives are:

**Lead Generation (native LinkedIn forms):** Contact details collected directly on LinkedIn without the user leaving the platform. Higher volume, lower friction. The downside is slightly lower lead quality compared to website form fills — the ease of submitting means some people do it without full intent.

**Website Conversions:** Drives clicks to your website and tracks conversions via the LinkedIn Insight Tag. Higher intent leads, lower volume. Works best when your landing page is strong and your offer is compelling.

**Website Visits / Brand Awareness:** Useful for the top-of-funnel phase — getting your brand in front of decision-makers at target companies over time. Does not generate leads directly but builds the recognition that makes your outbound and other channels more effective.

For most NZ B2B businesses starting on LinkedIn, Lead Generation campaigns are the right starting point — they generate leads at the lowest friction and give you the data to optimise quickly.

### Audience Targeting for NZ

New Zealand is a small market. LinkedIn's NZ user base is significantly smaller than Australia or the US, which means audience sizes can be limiting if you over-constrain targeting.

The targeting hierarchy to work through:

**Job title targeting:** Most precise, but use broad variations. "Marketing Manager," "Head of Marketing," "Marketing Director," "CMO" are all the same role at different company sizes. Build combined audiences that capture the full range.

**Job function + seniority:** Broader than job title targeting but good for generating volume when title targeting gives audiences under 5,000 in NZ. "Marketing function + Manager/Director/VP" is a workable combination.

**Company targeting:** If you have a specific account list (ABM approach), upload it and run campaigns only to employees of those companies. Highly efficient for enterprise-focused NZ B2B businesses.

**Industry targeting:** Useful for narrowing to verticals where you have the most success. But keep it relatively broad — too much stacking of targeting criteria quickly reduces NZ audiences below viable scale.

A practical NZ audience for most B2B campaigns: target role function, senior seniority, exclude students and entry-level, and set a loose geographic constraint (All of New Zealand, not just Auckland) to get audiences in the 20,000-80,000 range. Smaller than this and delivery becomes expensive and slow.

## Creative Format: What Works on LinkedIn in NZ

Format matters significantly on LinkedIn. The formats with the best performance for NZ B2B:

### Single Image Ads

The workhorse of LinkedIn advertising. High visual quality image (professional, not stock), short punchy headline, and a clear value proposition or hook. Scroll-stopping creative — something unexpected, a bold claim, or a number that implies insight.

For NZ audiences, local specificity improves performance. "How NZ businesses are cutting marketing costs by 40%" outperforms a generic global headline.

### Video Ads

Video on LinkedIn has better organic reach than static, and paid video ads get strong engagement when the content is genuinely valuable in the first 8 seconds. A 30-60 second video that delivers a real insight or a before/after result — not a product demo — performs best.

The key: hook in the first 3 seconds. LinkedIn autoplay is silent. The first visual frame and on-screen text need to be compelling without audio.

### Document Ads (PDF Carousels)

Lead magnets distributed as LinkedIn document ads — a checklist, a guide, a framework — get strong engagement because they deliver value directly in the feed. They also build brand authority through the content itself.

For NZ B2B businesses with genuinely useful content to share (industry benchmarks, how-to guides, frameworks), document ads are underused and often lower cost-per-engagement than image ads.

### Conversation Ads (InMail)

Sponsored messages sent to LinkedIn inboxes. Can be effective for event invitations or highly targeted outreach to specific job titles, but NZ professionals are increasingly immune to obviously automated InMail. Use sparingly and with genuine personalisation.

## What to Offer: The Cold Audience Problem

The most common mistake NZ B2B businesses make on LinkedIn is asking for too much too soon.

Running a campaign that says "book a demo" or "get in touch" to a cold audience that has never heard of your company generates very few leads at very high cost. Nobody books a demo with a stranger.

The offers that generate volume with cold LinkedIn audiences:

**Lead magnets:** Guides, checklists, frameworks, industry reports, templates. Something genuinely useful that gives the prospect value before asking for anything. The guide format works particularly well for NZ professional audiences.

**Event or webinar registration:** A specific, time-bounded event that solves a problem or addresses a question your target audience has. Lower commitment than a demo, and gives you a list of interested prospects to nurture.

**Free audit or assessment:** Works for agencies and consultancies. A free website audit, marketing audit, or financial health check. Specific enough to feel useful, open-ended enough that the conversation continues.

**Insight piece:** A specific finding or benchmark that is relevant to your audience. "We analysed 50 NZ SaaS businesses and found this..." creates curiosity and positions you as a thought leader before asking for attention.

## Measuring LinkedIn Advertising Performance in NZ

The metrics that actually matter for NZ LinkedIn campaigns:

**Cost per lead (CPL):** The primary efficiency metric. For NZ B2B, a CPL of $80-200 NZD is reasonable depending on industry and average deal value. If your deal is worth $100k, a $200 CPL is excellent. If your deal is worth $5k, it may not be sustainable.

**Lead quality rate:** Of all leads generated, what percentage are in your actual ICP (ideal customer profile)? LinkedIn targeting is not perfect — a percentage of leads will always be outside your target. Measuring this guides targeting refinements.

**Pipeline generated:** The real measure. Revenue pipeline attributable to LinkedIn campaigns over 90-180 days. This requires proper CRM tracking and is the number that justifies or kills the channel.

**View-through attribution:** B2B buyers typically see multiple touchpoints before converting. LinkedIn view-through attribution captures the influence of LinkedIn impressions on conversions that happen elsewhere. Important context for the channel's true contribution.

## Getting Started: The First 90 Days of LinkedIn Ads in NZ

For NZ B2B businesses running LinkedIn advertising for the first time:

**Month 1:** Build the foundation. Install the Insight Tag, create the lead gen form template, define 2-3 audiences, create 3-5 creative variants per audience, run with a modest budget ($50-100/day NZD) to generate learning data.

**Month 2:** Optimise on data. Which audiences are converting at the best CPL? Which creative formats and messages are getting the best engagement? Kill the low performers, scale the winners. Refine the lead magnet offer based on conversion rate.

**Month 3:** Scale what works. Increase budget on proven audiences and creative. Test the next campaign objective (if month 1-2 was lead gen, try website conversions on a refined audience). Begin building remarketing audiences from website visitors.

By month 3, you have enough data to know whether LinkedIn is a viable acquisition channel for your specific NZ business — and at what economics.
