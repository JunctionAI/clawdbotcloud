# Google Ads for Ecommerce NZ: The Complete Playbook for 2026

If you run an ecommerce store in New Zealand, Google Ads is likely your fastest path to revenue growth. While SEO builds long-term organic traffic and social media builds brand awareness, Google Ads delivers buyers to your product pages today.

But ecommerce Google Ads has become significantly more complex. Google Shopping has evolved into Performance Max. Smart Shopping is gone. Attribution is murkier. And competition from offshore retailers bidding on NZ keywords has intensified.

This guide cuts through the noise and explains how to run profitable Google Ads campaigns for your NZ ecommerce store in 2026.

---

## The Ecommerce Google Ads Ecosystem

For NZ online stores, there are four primary campaign types to understand:

### 1. Google Shopping (now within Performance Max)
Shopping ads show your product image, name, price, and store name when someone searches for a product you sell. They appear above text ads and receive significantly higher click-through rates for product searches.

Shopping ads are driven by your product feed (submitted via Google Merchant Center) rather than keywords you choose. Google's algorithm matches your products to relevant searches based on your product titles, descriptions, and other feed attributes.

**This makes your product feed the single most important element of your Shopping campaigns.**

### 2. Performance Max (PMax)
Performance Max is Google's all-in-one campaign type that serves across Search, Shopping, Display, YouTube, Gmail, and Maps. It uses AI to find converting customers across all channels.

PMax has largely replaced Smart Shopping campaigns and is now the dominant campaign type for ecommerce retailers. When set up correctly with good creative assets and clear conversion data, PMax can drive excellent ROAS.

### 3. Search Campaigns
Traditional keyword-targeted text ads. For ecommerce, Search campaigns work best for:
- High-intent brand keywords ("[your brand name]")
- Competitor keywords (conquest campaigns)
- Category-level searches where Shopping ads don't capture intent well
- "Best [product type] NZ" type research queries

### 4. Remarketing (via Display and YouTube)
Reaching people who've already visited your store but haven't purchased. In ecommerce, abandoned cart remarketing is one of the highest-ROAS activities available.

## Google Merchant Center: Getting Your Feed Right

Your product feed is the foundation of ecommerce Google Ads. Feed quality directly determines whether your products show for relevant searches and at competitive costs.

**Critical feed attributes:**

**Product titles.** This is your most important feed element. Include: primary keyword + brand + key specifications. For a running shoe: "Nike Air Zoom Pegasus 41 Running Shoes — Women's — Size 7–11 — Grey" beats "Nike Shoe."

**Product descriptions.** Include secondary keywords, key specifications, and unique selling points. These don't appear in Shopping ads directly, but Google uses them for keyword matching.

**Google product category.** Use Google's taxonomy precisely. The more specific, the better.

**GTIN/MPN.** For branded products, include barcodes (GTINs) and manufacturer part numbers. Products with GTINs are eligible for additional features and generally perform better.

**High-quality images.** White background product images for Shopping. Lifestyle images can be added as additional images for Performance Max.

**Accurate pricing and availability.** Feed disapprovals for price mismatches will tank your campaigns. Keep your feed synced with your store in real time.

## Performance Max Setup for NZ Ecommerce

Performance Max requires careful setup to perform well. Here's the framework:

**Asset groups.** Create separate asset groups for different product categories or audiences. Don't put all your products in one asset group — you lose the ability to understand what's working.

**Creative assets.** PMax serves across multiple channels. You need:
- 15 headlines (include product names, offers, NZ-specific trust signals)
- 5 descriptions
- Images (multiple aspect ratios: landscape, square, portrait)
- Logo
- Video (YouTube will auto-generate if you don't provide one — auto-generated videos are usually poor quality, so provide your own)

**Audience signals.** PMax works better with audience signals — hints to Google about who your best customers are. Provide:
- Your customer list (email addresses of past purchasers)
- Website visitors (via remarketing tag)
- In-market audiences relevant to your products

**Budget.** PMax needs data to learn. Start with a budget that generates at least 30–50 conversions per month to give Google's algorithm enough to work with.

## ROAS Targets for NZ Ecommerce

Return on Ad Spend (ROAS) is the primary metric for ecommerce Google Ads. Calculate it as: Revenue ÷ Ad Spend.

**Setting realistic ROAS targets:**

Your target ROAS must account for your gross margin. If you're selling products with a 40% gross margin, you need at least 2.5x ROAS just to break even on the margin before accounting for other costs. A profitable ROAS target might be 4–6x depending on your business economics.

**NZ ecommerce benchmarks:**
- Fashion/apparel: 3–5x ROAS
- Electronics: 4–8x ROAS (lower margins, need higher ROAS to be profitable)
- Homewares/furniture: 4–7x ROAS
- Health/beauty: 3–6x ROAS
- Sporting goods: 4–7x ROAS

These are general benchmarks. Your actual target depends on your specific margins and business model.

## Search Campaigns for Ecommerce: When and How

While Shopping and PMax handle most ecommerce traffic, Search campaigns have specific use cases:

**Brand campaigns.** Always run a brand campaign to protect your branded searches. Without it, competitors can bid on your brand name and steal clicks from people specifically looking for you. Brand campaigns typically deliver 10–20x ROAS.

**Category + "best" keywords.** Searches like "best running shoes NZ" or "best standing desk under $500 NZ" often indicate someone in active research mode who is close to purchasing. Search campaigns let you appear for these queries with tailored ad copy.

**Competitor campaigns.** Bidding on competitor brand names is legal and often effective. Serve ads when someone searches your competitor with messaging that highlights your differentiation.

## Remarketing: Recovering Lost Revenue

Up to 97% of visitors leave your ecommerce store without purchasing. Remarketing brings them back.

**Abandoned cart remarketing** is the highest-value remarketing segment. Someone who added a product to their cart and left has clearly indicated purchase intent. Serve them highly relevant ads featuring exactly what they abandoned, potentially with a time-limited offer.

**Product viewer remarketing** targets people who viewed specific products but didn't add to cart. Serve ads featuring those products.

**Past customer remarketing** encourages repeat purchases. Segment by time since last purchase and product category for maximum relevance.

**Setup:** Install Google's remarketing tag across your site (or use your CMS integration with Google Ads) and create audience lists for each segment.

## Tracking and Attribution for Ecommerce

Accurate conversion tracking is non-negotiable for ecommerce Google Ads. You need:

**Google Ads conversion tracking** with purchase value passed (not just a conversion count). This tells Google the revenue generated by each conversion.

**Enhanced conversions.** Google's enhanced conversions use first-party data to improve attribution accuracy, particularly in a world of cookie restrictions and cross-device journeys.

**GA4 ecommerce tracking.** Google Analytics 4 provides richer attribution data, customer journey analysis, and cohort reporting that Google Ads alone doesn't offer.

**Regular attribution audits.** Check for double-counting, ensure your Merchant Center account is linked, and verify that revenue figures in Google Ads roughly match your store's actual revenue from that traffic source.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Not optimising your product feed.** Most NZ ecommerce stores launch Shopping campaigns without investing in feed quality. Vague product titles, missing GTINs, and poor descriptions leave significant performance on the table.

**Setting ROAS targets too high initially.** If you set a 10x ROAS target when campaigns are new, Google's bidding algorithm will be so conservative it won't generate meaningful traffic. Start with a realistic target and adjust as data accumulates.

**Ignoring brand campaigns.** Competitors are bidding on your brand. If you're not running brand protection campaigns, you're handing them sales.

**Not segmenting by product margin.** Selling both high-margin and low-margin products in the same campaign? You need separate campaigns with different ROAS targets. Averaging them together means you're overspending on low-margin products and underspending on high-margin ones.

**Neglecting Performance Max creative.** PMax auto-generates creative if you provide minimal assets. The results are usually poor. Invest in quality headlines, descriptions, and images for every asset group.
