# How to Decide Where to Focus Time & Attention
## Compiled from Thought Leaders - 2026-01-31

Tom's question: "What should be the thought process when deciding what to focus time and attention on?"

---

## 1. Naval Ravikant - The Four Types of Luck Framework

**Decision filter: Which type of luck am I creating?**

1. **Blind luck** - Random, uncontrollable (ignore this)
2. **Hustle luck** - Creating opportunities through motion and energy
3. **Preparation luck** - Spotting opportunities through skill/expertise
4. **Character luck** - Building reputation so opportunities find YOU

**Key principle:**
> "Build your character in a certain way and then your character becomes your destiny."

**Application to focus:**
- Focus on work that builds REPUTATION and UNIQUE CHARACTER
- "Escape competition through authenticity" - be so uniquely you that luck finds you
- Work where you can become **irreplaceable** (not doing set roles anyone can learn)

**The wealth creation test:**
> "Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep."

Ask: Is this work creating **leverage**? Or just renting out my time?
- If inputs = outputs (more hours = more money), wrong focus
- If disconnected (1 hour can create massive value OR zero), right focus

**Three ingredients for great work:**
1. Natural ability
2. Practice  
3. Effort

You need ALL THREE. Focus on areas where you have natural ability + genuine interest.

## 2. Paul Graham - Working Hard on the Right Thing

**Decision filter: Am I aiming toward the center?**

**The Hard Core Principle:**
> "Many problems have a hard core at the center, surrounded by easier stuff at the edges. Working hard means aiming toward the center to the extent you can."

**Two levels:**
1. **Daily:** Aim toward the hardest part of current project (don't just do easy peripheral tasks)
2. **Lifetime:** Aim toward the most ambitious problems in your field

**The Interest Test (Most Important):**
> "The best test of whether it's worthwhile to work on something is whether you find it interesting."

Why this works:
- You're the one doing the work - you're best positioned to judge
- Deep interest makes people work harder than any discipline can
- Honesty with yourself is CRITICAL here

**On figuring out what to work on:**
- Some people converge fast (Mozart knew as a child)
- Others never converge (Newton tried alchemy, theology, math, physics)
- For the latter: figuring out what to work on IS PART OF the work itself
- It's "an ongoing part of it, like one of a set of simultaneous equations"

**When to switch:**
> "If you're working hard but not getting good enough results, you should switch."

But give yourself time to get going. The tricky part: how much time?

**Quality limits:**
- Different types of work have different hour limits before quality declines
- PG: ~5 hours/day for hard writing/programming
- Startups: could work all the time (driven by fear of failure)
- Find YOUR limit by crossing it, then adjusting

**Honesty requirement:**
- Notice when you're being lazy
- Notice when you're working too hard
- Working too hard ≠ admirable, it = worse results + showing off

## 3. Alex Hormozi - $100M Offers Framework

**Decision filter: What creates the most value in the least time?**

**The Grand Slam Offer Formula:**
What you're selling (or building) should be:
1. Dream outcome (what they really want)
2. Perceived likelihood of achievement (do they believe it works?)
3. Time delay (how fast?)
4. Effort & sacrifice (how easy?)

**Applied to YOUR time allocation:**

Ask: Which activity creates:
- **Highest dream outcome** for you/your clients?
- **Highest certainty** of success?
- **Fastest results**?
- **Least friction/effort**?

**The Compounding Principle:**
> "Do $10K/month work, not $10K one-time work."

Focus on:
- Recurring revenue > one-time projects
- Scalable systems > manual labor
- Compounding assets > consumption

**Velocity over perfection:**
- Volume → learn faster → refine → compound
- Kill fast (if something doesn't work in 60 days, move on)
- Double down on what works

## 4. Peter Thiel - The One Thing Question

**Decision filter: What's the ONE thing that matters most?**

**The Power Law:**
> "One market will be better than all others. One distribution strategy will dominate. One time and decision will matter far more than all others combined."

**Applied to focus:**
- What's the ONE thing that, if you got it right, would make everything else easier or unnecessary?
- Don't spread bets - concentrate on the highest-leverage opportunity

**The Contrarian Test:**
> "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

Focus on things where:
- You have a secret (insight others don't have)
- You can build a monopoly (not compete in commoditized markets)
- You're going from 0 to 1 (creating new), not 1 to n (copying)

## 5. Clayton Christensen - Jobs to Be Done

**Decision filter: What job needs doing that nothing else does well?**

**The JTBD Framework:**
People (or YOU) "hire" solutions to make progress in specific circumstances.

Ask yourself:
- What "job" am I trying to get done in my life right now?
- What progress am I trying to make?
- Which activities actually accomplish that job?
- Which are just motions that don't serve the real job?

**Example for Tom:**
Job: "Get to $400k/year revenue while building toward $1M and freedom"

Activities that serve the job:
- Closing Clawdbot clients (direct revenue)
- Launching apps (testing for winners)
- Building PG relationship (potential for large contract)

Activities that DON'T serve the job:
- Perfecting systems that don't generate revenue yet
- Research that doesn't lead to action
- Networking that doesn't convert to business

## 6. Cal Newport - Deep Work Principles

**Decision filter: Does this require deep focus, or is it shallow?**

**The Deep vs Shallow Test:**

**Deep work** = 
- Cognitively demanding
- Creates new value
- Hard to replicate
- Requires sustained focus

**Shallow work** =
- Logistical/admin
- Doesn't create new value
- Anyone could do it
- Can do while distracted

**Time allocation rule:**
Maximize deep work hours. Batch/eliminate shallow work.

**The Craftsman Approach:**
> "Focus on what you can offer the world, not what the world offers you."

Don't ask: "What passion should I follow?"
Ask: "What can I get REALLY GOOD at that's valuable?"

**Skill compounds:**
- Rare + valuable skills = career capital
- Career capital = ability to shape your career

## 7. Warren Buffett - The 25/5 Rule

**Decision filter: What should I absolutely NOT do?**

**The Process:**
1. List your top 25 goals/priorities
2. Circle the top 5
3. **The other 20 become your "avoid at all costs" list**

Why?
- They're ATTRACTIVE enough to distract you
- But NOT important enough to deserve focus
- They will kill your ability to achieve the top 5

**Applied:**
Don't just decide what to focus on.
Decide what to actively AVOID.

---

## 8. James Clear - The Aggregation of Marginal Gains

**Decision filter: Does this compound over time?**

**1% Better Framework:**
Small improvements, repeated consistently, compound exponentially.

**Four Laws of Behavior Change (applied to focus):**
1. **Make it obvious** - What deserves attention should be visible
2. **Make it attractive** - Work on things you're genuinely drawn to
3. **Make it easy** - Reduce friction on high-value activities
4. **Make it satisfying** - Track progress to maintain motivation

**The Plateau of Latent Potential:**
Results lag behind effort. What feels like wasted time is actually stored potential.

Focus on systems that compound:
- Learning (gets easier as you know more)
- Relationships (get stronger over time)
- Reputation (builds on itself)
- Assets (earn while you sleep)

## SYNTHESIS: The Meta-Framework

### When deciding where to focus time and attention:

**1. FILTER: Interest + Ability**
- Deep interest? (PG's test)
- Natural ability? (Naval's ingredients)
- Both? → This is your domain

**2. FILTER: Leverage + Compounding**
- Disconnected inputs/outputs? (Naval)
- Builds assets that earn while you sleep? (Naval)
- Compounds over time? (James Clear)
- Yes? → This creates wealth

**3. FILTER: Unique Character**
- Are you becoming irreplaceable? (Naval)
- Building reputation/brand? (Naval)
- Creating your own category? (Thiel)
- Yes? → Luck will find you

**4. FILTER: The Hard Core**
- Aiming toward center, not edges? (PG)
- Most ambitious problem you can handle? (PG)
- The ONE thing that matters most? (Thiel)
- Yes? → You're working on what counts

**5. FILTER: Quality Threshold**
- Honest about whether it's working? (PG)
- Getting results, or just busy? (Hormozi)
- Deep work or shallow work? (Cal Newport)
- Adjust or switch accordingly

**6. FILTER: Explicit Avoidance**
- What are you NOT doing? (Buffett 25/5)
- What attractive distractions will you reject?
- What jobs don't serve your goal? (Christensen)
